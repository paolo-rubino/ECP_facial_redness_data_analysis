### Analysis and Discussion

**Evaluation of Hypotheses and Interpretation of Results**
The primary objective of this study was to conceptually replicate previous research on the influence of facial redness on emotion perception, testing four specific hypotheses. To robustly account for the ordinal nature of the Likert-scale data, hypotheses were evaluated using both frequentist linear mixed-effects models and a Bayesian cumulative probit model. Overall, the data failed to support the pre-registered directional hypotheses regarding the amplifying effects of facial redness, though they successfully corroborated our anticipated null effect for fear. 

Before evaluating the primary hypotheses, a manipulation check confirmed that participants reliably distinguished the presented emotions. Ratings for congruent emotion-face pairings ($M = 3.615$) were vastly higher than for incongruent pairings ($M = 1.623$), $t(52) = 23.58$, $p < .001$, $d = 3.24$, validating the core experimental design across our 53 participants and 848 congruent observations.

Contrary to Hypothesis 1, artificially increasing the facial redness of angry faces did not significantly amplify their perceived anger intensity ($M_{Red} = 3.94$, $M_{Normal} = 3.86$, mean difference = 0.085, 95% CI [-0.088, 0.257]; frequentist $t(52) = 0.96$, $p = 0.339$, $d = 0.13$). The Bayesian model confirmed this, as the 95% High-Density Interval (HDI) for the redness effect included zero (HDI: [-0.170, 0.397]). Similarly, the anticipated "red-approach" enhancement for happy faces (Hypothesis 3) was not observed ($M_{Red} = 4.36$, $M_{Normal} = 4.29$, mean difference = 0.066, 95% CI [-0.079, 0.211]; $t(52) = 0.90$, $p = 0.375$; Bayesian interaction HDI: [-0.451, 0.388]). Furthermore, Hypothesis 4 predicted that superimposing facial redness on a neutral face would artificially inflate baseline anger ratings. This did not occur; instead, ratings trended marginally—though non-significantly—in the opposite direction ($M_{Red} = 1.79$, $M_{Normal} = 1.92$, mean difference = -0.123, 95% CI [-0.262, 0.017]; $t(52) = -1.72$, $p = 0.091$; Bayesian interaction HDI: [-0.662, 0.175]). However, as predicted by Hypothesis 2, facial redness did not systematically influence the perceived intensity of fearful expressions ($M_{Red} = 2.58$, $M_{Normal} = 2.66$, mean difference = -0.085, 95% CI [-0.313, 0.143]; $t(52) = -0.73$, $p = 0.469$, $d = -0.10$; Bayesian interaction HDI: [-0.558, 0.249]). 

As illustrated in the **Main Effects Bar Chart**, the visual comparison of Red versus Normal ratings across these four conditions clearly demonstrates this lack of effect, with virtually identical bar heights and heavily overlapping 95% confidence intervals. The **Interaction Line Plot** further illustrates this; the almost perfectly parallel lines between the Red and Normal conditions demonstrate a lack of significant interaction, suggesting that the human visual system processes structural emotion independently of these subtle color shifts.

Furthermore, as visualized in the **Posterior Predictive Check**, the Bayesian model indicated an excellent fit for the ordinal data. The simulated model predictions tightly clustered around and traced the exact shape of the observed data distribution, proving the model successfully captured how participants used the 1-to-5 rating scale, thereby reinforcing the high reliability of these null findings. Psychologically, these results suggest that when observers are presented with prototypical, unambiguous facial muscle configurations, structural facial muscle information is so dominant that subtle physiological color cues provide almost no additive value.

**Demographic Profile and Sample Characteristics**
While not part of the experimental hypotheses, the demographic data provided essential context for the sample's characteristics. As shown in the **Gender Distribution Pie Chart**, the sample was overwhelmingly female (94.4%), which is a common skew in university-based social science recruitment. The **Age Distribution Plot**, now utilizing 20 bins for higher granularity, revealed a highly concentrated sample of young adults, with the vast majority of participants falling between 19 and 25 years of age. Finally, the **Occupation Pie Chart** confirmed that the most predominant class was "EPFL student," representing a significant majority of the sample compared to other student or professional backgrounds. This demographic homogeneity suggests that the observed null effects are consistent within a young, university-age population, but limits generalizability to older or more diverse populations.

**Replication Outcomes: Similarities and Differences**
Our findings represent a highly successful conceptual replication of the target study by Wolf et al. (2021). In foundational literature, the "red-anger" heuristic was considered a robust phenomenon, positing that humans universally rely on facial redness as a perceptual cue for approach-oriented emotions. However, Wolf et al. (2021) argued that those older effects were likely artifacts of demand characteristics.

As shown in the **Replication Comparison Plot**, our effect sizes ($d$ ranging from -0.24 to 0.13) align with the near-zero effects reported by the original authors. While our study found slightly larger magnitudes for H1 and H4 than the original study, the confidence intervals remained overlapping with zero. By utilizing a subtle, realistic color manipulation and a block-randomized design that masked the study's intent, our results perfectly mirror Wolf et al. (2021): we found no credible evidence for the classic "red-anger" or "red-approach" effects.

A notable difference lies in our exploratory findings. While investigating all pairwise emotion ratings—visualized in the **Exploratory Differences Heatmap**—we discovered a highly specific effect: increased facial redness significantly *reduced* the perceived intensity of *disgust* when participants viewed angry faces ($\Delta M = -0.37$, $p = 0.002$, $d = -0.45$). This stands out distinctly as a strong negative mean difference in the heatmap matrix. This suggests redness might not act as an intensifier, but as a disambiguator: because anger and disgust share overlapping facial muscle configurations, the addition of redness may act as a biological signal that firmly categorizes the expression as anger, thereby actively downregulating the perception of disgust.

**Limitations and Future Directions**
While this replication was successful, several methodological limitations must be acknowledged. First, our stimulus set exclusively featured female targets. Future replications must ensure a balanced presentation of male and female targets to rule out potential gender-based interaction effects in how facial coloration is perceived.

Second, regarding the theoretical construct, this study relied on static images. In everyday interaction, physiological changes like flushing are dynamic, temporal processes. Future research should utilize dynamically manipulated video stimuli where the facial hue visibly flushes over time, as humans are likely more sensitive to the *rate of change* in coloration than static hues.

Finally, future directions should shift toward using *ambiguous* stimuli. If facial redness acts primarily as a disambiguating cue—as suggested by our exploratory finding regarding the suppression of disgust—researchers should test the effects of redness on faces morphed between anger and disgust. Under those conditions of perceptual uncertainty, the subtle cue of facial redness might systematically tip the scales of perception.
