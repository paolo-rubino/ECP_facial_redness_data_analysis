### Analysis and Discussion

**Evaluation of Hypotheses and Interpretation of Results**
The primary objective of this study was to conceptually replicate previous research on the influence of facial redness on emotion perception, testing four specific hypotheses. To robustly account for the ordinal nature of the Likert-scale data, hypotheses were evaluated using both frequentist linear mixed-effects models and a Bayesian cumulative probit model. Overall, the data failed to support the pre-registered directional hypotheses regarding the amplifying effects of facial redness, though they successfully corroborated our anticipated null effect for fear. 

Before evaluating the primary hypotheses, a manipulation check confirmed that participants reliably distinguished the presented emotions. Ratings for congruent emotion-face pairings ($M = 3.615$) were vastly higher than for incongruent pairings ($M = 1.623$), $t(52) = 23.58$, $p < .001$, $d = 3.24$, validating the core experimental design across our 53 participants and 848 congruent observations.

Contrary to Hypothesis 1, artificially increasing the facial redness of angry faces did not significantly amplify their perceived anger intensity ($M_{Red} = 3.94$, $M_{Normal} = 3.86$, mean difference = 0.085, 95% CI [-0.088, 0.257]; frequentist $t(52) = 0.96$, $p = 0.339$, $d = 0.13$). The Bayesian model confirmed this, as the 95% High-Density Interval (HDI) for the redness effect included zero (HDI: [-0.170, 0.397]). Similarly, the anticipated "red-approach" enhancement for happy faces (Hypothesis 3) was not observed ($M_{Red} = 4.36$, $M_{Normal} = 4.29$, mean difference = 0.066, 95% CI [-0.079, 0.211]; $t(52) = 0.90$, $p = 0.375$; Bayesian interaction HDI: [-0.451, 0.388]). Furthermore, Hypothesis 4 predicted that superimposing facial redness on a neutral face would artificially inflate baseline anger ratings. This did not occur; instead, ratings trended marginally—though non-significantly—in the opposite direction ($M_{Red} = 1.79$, $M_{Normal} = 1.92$, mean difference = -0.123, 95% CI [-0.262, 0.017]; $t(52) = -1.72$, $p = 0.091$; Bayesian interaction HDI: [-0.662, 0.175]). However, as predicted by Hypothesis 2, facial redness did not systematically influence the perceived intensity of fearful expressions ($M_{Red} = 2.58$, $M_{Normal} = 2.66$, mean difference = -0.085, 95% CI [-0.313, 0.143]; $t(52) = -0.73$, $p = 0.469$, $d = -0.10$; Bayesian interaction HDI: [-0.558, 0.249]). As illustrated in the **Main Effects Bar Chart (Figure 1)**, the visual comparison of Red versus Normal ratings across these four conditions clearly demonstrates this lack of effect, with virtually identical bar heights and heavily overlapping 95% confidence intervals.

Furthermore, as visualized in the **Posterior Predictive Check (Figure 2)**, the Bayesian model indicated an excellent fit for the ordinal data. The simulated model predictions tightly clustered around and traced the exact shape of the observed data distribution, proving the model successfully captured how participants used the 1-to-5 rating scale, thereby reinforcing the high reliability of these null findings. Psychologically, these results suggest that when observers are presented with prototypical, unambiguous facial muscle configurations, subtle shifts in skin coloration do not provide additive informational value. The facial action coding alone appears to drive the intensity rating, rendering the static color cue perceptually redundant.

**Demographic Profile and Sample Characteristics**
While not directly part of the experimental hypotheses, the demographic data provided essential context for the generalizability of these findings. As shown in the **Gender Distribution Plot (Figure 4)**, the sample was predominantly female, reflecting a common trend in university-based social science recruitment. The **Age Distribution Plot (Figure 5)** revealed a highly concentrated sample of young adults, with the vast majority of participants falling between 19 and 25 years of age. This demographic homogeneity suggests that the observed null effects of facial redness are highly consistent within a young, university-age population, though it leaves open the question of whether older adults—who may have different perceptual sensitivities or social experiences—would react differently to these color cues.

**Replication Outcomes: Similarities and Differences**
Our findings represent a highly successful conceptual replication of the target study by Wolf et al. (2021). In foundational literature, the "red-anger" heuristic was considered a robust phenomenon, positing that humans universally rely on facial redness as a perceptual cue for approach-oriented emotions. However, Wolf et al. (2021) argued that those older effects were likely artifacts of demand characteristics, driven by experimental designs that flashed extreme red faces back-to-back, forcing participants to react to the color change. 

By utilizing a more subtle, realistic color manipulation and a block-randomized design that masked the study's intent, our results perfectly mirror Wolf et al. (2021): we found absolutely no credible evidence for the classic "red-anger" or "red-approach" effects. 

A notable difference between our study and the original paper lies in our exploratory findings. While investigating all pairwise emotion ratings—visualized comprehensively in the **Exploratory Differences Heatmap (Figure 3)**—we discovered a highly specific, localized effect: increased facial redness significantly *reduced* the perceived intensity of *disgust* when participants viewed angry faces ($\Delta M = -0.37$, frequentist $p = 0.002$, $d = -0.45$). This stands out distinctly as a strong negative mean difference in the heatmap matrix. Another exploratory observation indicated a slight reduction in surprised ratings for neutral faces when redness was added ($\Delta M = -0.13$, $p = 0.047$, $d = -0.28$). This suggests an intriguing alternative mechanism: while redness might not artificially *inflate* the target emotion (anger), it may serve as a disambiguating cue that suppresses competing, visually similar secondary emotions. Because anger and disgust share overlapping facial muscle configurations (e.g., furrowed brows), the addition of redness may act as a signal that firmly categorizes the expression as anger, thereby actively downregulating the perception of disgust.

**Limitations and Future Directions**
While this replication was successful, several methodological limitations must be acknowledged. First, an artifact of our data collection resulted in a stimulus set that exclusively featured female targets. Consequently, target gender had to be dropped from the statistical models to prevent singular matrix errors. Future replications must ensure a balanced presentation of male and female targets to rule out potential gender-based interaction effects in how facial coloration is perceived.

Second, regarding the theoretical construct, this study—like the vast majority of literature in this field—relied on static images. In everyday human interaction, physiological changes such as blushing or flushing are inherently dynamic, temporal processes. Humans may not be evolutionarily wired to evaluate the absolute static color of a face against a hypothetical baseline; rather, we are likely sensitive to the *rate of change* in coloration over time. Future research should abandon static photography and utilize dynamically manipulated video stimuli where the facial hue visibly flushes over a span of milliseconds to seconds. 

Finally, future directions should shift away from using prototypical emotional expressions. If facial redness acts primarily as a disambiguating cue—as suggested by our exploratory finding regarding the suppression of disgust—researchers should test the effects of redness on *ambiguous* stimuli. Utilizing faces that are morphed 50/50 between anger and disgust, or anger and fear, would force participants into a state of perceptual uncertainty. Under those specific conditions, the subtle cue of facial redness might systematically tip the scales of perception, revealing how physiological color integration functions when facial muscle movements alone are insufficient for emotion categorization.
