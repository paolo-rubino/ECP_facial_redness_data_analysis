The statistical analyses conducted to evaluate the influence of facial redness on emotion perception largely failed to support the pre-registered directional hypotheses, though they successfully corroborated the anticipated null effect for fear. These results align closely with the skeptical conclusions of the replication target (Wolf et al., 2021). Contrary to the hypothesized "red-anger" amplification effect (H1), artificially increasing the facial redness of angry faces did not significantly increase their perceived anger intensity ($M_{Red} = 3.94$, $M_{Normal} = 3.86$; $t(52) = 0.96$, $p = 0.339$, $d = 0.13$). Similarly, facial redness failed to produce the anticipated enhancement for happy faces (H3; $t(52) = 0.90$, $p = 0.375$, $d = 0.12$), nor did it increase the baseline perception of anger when superimposed on neutral expressions (H4; $M_{Red} = 1.79$, $M_{Normal} = 1.92$; $t(52) = -1.72$, $p = 0.091$, $d = -0.24$), which instead trended marginally in the opposite direction. However, as predicted by Hypothesis 2, facial redness did not systematically influence the perceived intensity of fearful expressions ($t(52) = -0.73$, $p = 0.469$, $d = -0.10$). Interestingly, while the primary targeted enhancements were absent, exploratory pairwise analyses across all emotion ratings revealed an unexpected, localized effect: increased facial redness significantly reduced the perceived intensity of *disgust* on angry faces ($\Delta M = -0.37$, $p = 0.002$, $d = -0.45$). Taken together, these results challenge the generalizability of earlier literature that posited facial redness as a robust, systematic perceptual cue for anger or approach-related emotions, suggesting instead that its main effects on target emotion recognition are negligible when tested with subtle manipulations.
